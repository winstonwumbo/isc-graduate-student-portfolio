Area of Specialization

My expertise lies at the intersection of equity-centered mathematics curriculum design and the study of mathematical identity among post-secondary and adult learners. What unifies these two threads is a single animating question: how do the structures we build into mathematics instruction — the problems we choose, the sequences we design, the norms we establish — either invite students into mathematical thinking or quietly signal that they don't belong there? I work to understand that question rigorously and to answer it practically.

My work spans three interconnected domains. The first is curriculum design: I develop instructional materials, assessment sequences, and unit frameworks for college mathematics and workforce training contexts, with equity and access treated as design requirements rather than afterthoughts. The second is direct instruction and tutoring: I bring years of experience supporting students across the full range of undergraduate mathematics, from developmental arithmetic through calculus, with particular skill in meeting students where they are and building from there. The third is research: I investigate how students — especially adult learners and those in non-traditional educational pathways — construct their identities as mathematical thinkers, and how intentional instructional choices can shift that construction in meaningful ways.

Best Example of Work

The project I am most proud of is a curriculum redesign I undertook within Penn State's Workforce Development Programs, which also forms the empirical core of my master's thesis. It began with a straightforward observation: the mathematics curriculum used in our workforce credentialing program had been assembled piecemeal from developmental college materials and bore almost no relationship to the actual quantitative demands of the industries our students were entering. More troublingly, it was organized in a way that consistently positioned students as deficient — built around remediation sequences defined by what they lacked rather than by what they were trying to accomplish.

I redesigned three instructional units from the ground up using a backward design framework anchored in the real mathematical tasks workers encounter in supply chain logistics, healthcare support, and the building trades. Each unit opened with an authentic workplace scenario, developed the underlying mathematical concepts through that context rather than in abstraction before application, and embedded multiple low-stakes formative assessment checkpoints that gave students clear visibility into their own progress. I also rewrote the framing language throughout — the instructions, the learning goals, the feedback prompts — to reflect what students were gaining rather than cataloguing what they were still missing.

The results were meaningful in both quantitative and qualitative terms. Completion rates for the redesigned units were noticeably higher than historical averages for the same content, and post-unit surveys showed marked increases in students' self-reported confidence and sense of relevance. But the finding I return to most often came from the narrative interviews I conducted as part of my thesis data collection: several students, unprompted, described the curriculum as the first time mathematics had ever felt like it was written for them. That is the outcome I am working toward — not just better pass rates, but a genuinely different relationship between students and the subject.

This project represents my expertise not as a list of skills but as a way of working: beginning with the learner, interrogating the assumptions embedded in existing materials, and holding rigor and belonging as equally non-negotiable standards.
