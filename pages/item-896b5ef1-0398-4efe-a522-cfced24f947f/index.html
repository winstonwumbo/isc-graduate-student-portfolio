My professional expertise spans three interconnected domains: equity-centered mathematics curriculum design, post-secondary and workforce mathematics instruction, and the study of mathematical identity development among non-traditional learners. These areas reflect both the practical knowledge I have built through years of direct instruction and tutoring, and the scholarly grounding I have developed through graduate study at Penn State University.

Curriculum Vitae

Areas of Expertise

  • Equity-Centered Curriculum Design: I design instructional sequences, assessments, and learning materials that center student belonging and access alongside mathematical rigor, with particular focus on developmental and gateway mathematics contexts.
  • Post-Secondary & Workforce Mathematics Instruction: I teach and tutor college mathematics and adult workforce learners across a range of courses from developmental arithmetic through calculus, with expertise in differentiated instruction and formative assessment.
  • Mathematical Identity & Engagement: I investigate how students — particularly adult learners and those in workforce programs — construct or reconstruct their identities as mathematical thinkers, and how instructional environments mediate that process.
  • Pedagogical Content Knowledge: I bring deep command of undergraduate mathematics content (algebra, pre-calculus, statistics, calculus) and the research-informed pedagogical strategies most effective for those domains.
  • Supplemental Instruction & Peer Learning: I design and facilitate peer-assisted learning structures that improve outcomes in high-enrollment gateway courses.

Future Professional Aspirations

My professional aspirations are shaped by a clear sense of the kind of work I want to do and the kind of scholar-practitioner I am becoming. In the near term, my goal is to complete my master's thesis and contribute original, peer-reviewed scholarship to the mathematics education literature — specifically in the areas of adult mathematical identity and equity-centered curriculum design.

Looking further forward, I aspire to doctoral study in mathematics education, with the intention of pursuing a faculty position that allows me to sustain an active research agenda while remaining connected to teacher preparation and practitioner development. I am especially interested in programs that bridge university-based research and community college or workforce education contexts — environments that have historically been underrepresented in the scholarly literature despite serving a large and diverse student population.

I also aspire to contribute to the design of large-scale professional development programs for mathematics instructors in post-secondary and workforce settings — translating research on identity, equity, and curriculum into practical tools that working educators can actually use.

Self-Identified Boundaries

Quantitative & Mixed-Methods Research

My methodological training has been predominantly qualitative. While I have completed graduate coursework in regression analysis and contributed to program evaluation work involving quantitative outcome data, I do not yet have the depth of expertise required to independently design or lead a mixed-methods or quantitative study. I recognize this as a meaningful limitation, particularly given that funding agencies and many peer-reviewed venues expect researchers to command multiple methodological traditions.

Knowledge of K–12 Classroom Contexts

My practical experience is concentrated in post-secondary and adult learning settings. I have limited direct experience with K–12 classrooms, and my knowledge of elementary and middle school mathematics pedagogy — while informed by my graduate coursework — has not been tested through practice. This represents a gap relative to many mathematics education researchers, who draw on deep K–12 experience to inform their scholarship.
