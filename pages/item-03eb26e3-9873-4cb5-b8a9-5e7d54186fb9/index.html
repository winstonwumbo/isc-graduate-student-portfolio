Equity-Centered Curriculum Design

My work in mathematics education is anchored in the belief that curriculum is not neutral—it communicates who belongs, who is expected to succeed, and whose ways of thinking are valued. As both an instructor and curriculum designer in college and workforce settings, I have focused on developing instructional materials that center access alongside rigor. My experience designing curricula for adult learners, many of whom return to mathematics after years of disengagement, has reinforced the importance of intentional sequencing, transparent expectations, and meaningful context.

In practice, my curriculum design process begins with backward planning: identifying the core mathematical ideas students should understand and the ways they should be able to demonstrate that understanding. From there, I design tasks that build conceptual understanding through multiple representations and scaffolded progression. I prioritize problems that are grounded in authentic contexts—particularly those relevant to workforce pathways—so that students can immediately see the utility and relevance of mathematical reasoning.

Equity, in this context, is operationalized through design decisions. I incorporate principles from Universal Design for Learning by offering multiple entry points into tasks, allowing students to engage through visual, symbolic, and verbal reasoning. I also attend to the language of mathematics instruction, making expectations explicit and reducing reliance on implicit academic norms that may disadvantage first-generation or non-traditional learners.
